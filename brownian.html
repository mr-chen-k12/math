Understanding Brownian Motion

What is Brownian Motion?

Brownian motion is the continuous, random, zig-zag motion of microscopic particles suspended in a fluid (a liquid or a gas). It is named after the botanist Robert Brown, who first observed this erratic movement while looking at pollen grains in water under a microscope in 1827.

The movement is caused by the invisible, rapid collisions of the fluid's molecules against the suspended particle. Because the fluid molecules are in constant thermal motion, they strike the larger particle from all sides at random, causing it to "walk" randomly.

The Math Behind It

In 1905, Albert Einstein published a paper mathematically describing Brownian motion, proving the existence of atoms and molecules. He showed that the mean squared displacement (the average distance squared that a particle travels) is proportional to time.

This is expressed by the diffusion equation:

$$ \langle x^2 \rangle = 2Dt $$

Where:

  • \( \langle x^2 \rangle \) is the mean squared displacement.
  • \( D \) is the diffusion coefficient (which depends on temperature and fluid viscosity).
  • \( t \) is time.
