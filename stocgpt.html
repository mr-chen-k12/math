Stochastic Calculus: Random Motion Over Time

Stochastic calculus is calculus for quantities that move partly by rules and partly by randomness. In finance, one famous model is Geometric Brownian Motion, which is used to simulate possible asset price paths.

Model Controls

This is the drift: the general upward or downward tendency.

This controls how wild the randomness is. Higher volatility means wider possible paths.

The key equation

\[ dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t \]

\(S_t\) is the price. The \( \mu S_t dt \) part is the smooth drift. The \( \sigma S_t dW_t \) part is the random Brownian-motion shock.

1

Brownian Motion

Brownian motion, often written as \(W_t\), is a continuous random path. It wiggles unpredictably, but its statistical behavior can be modeled.

\[ W_{t+\Delta t}-W_t \sim N(0,\Delta t) \]
2

Stochastic Calculus

Regular calculus handles smooth change. Stochastic calculus handles change with random noise added at every tiny moment.

\[ dX_t = a(X_t,t)dt + b(X_t,t)dW_t \]
3

GBM Price Model

Geometric Brownian Motion keeps prices positive because the randomness acts proportionally on the current price.

\[ S_{t+\Delta t}=S_t e^{(\mu-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\Delta t+\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}Z} \]
4

Risk Simulation

One future path is not enough. Many random paths help estimate risk, possible gains, and possible losses.

\[ Z \sim N(0,1) \]
